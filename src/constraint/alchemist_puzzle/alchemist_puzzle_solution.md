# FORMULA IV-C — Solution

## The story

This puzzle was written when I was running a series of adventures for John that all had puzzles. He and I shared a love of puzzles, and he was amazingly fast at solving him. I was truly envious of not just the speed, but the variety of puzzles that came naturally to him. Logic puzzles, word puzzles, image searches, even connect the dots were done with such speed and accuracy.  I looked forward to trying to stump him, but any "fair" puzzle never did.

The adventures we were running had puzzles that were just too easy, I solved them instantly, so I knew that John would see no difficulty. So I tweaked them to be harder. I kept the "regular" version for the folks to think on, and would give him the harder one. This one he missed, and while I think he would have solved it, it might have taken him a few extra minutes. Or maybe it wouldn't have. 

 Also attached is the program that I wrote, which was how I was generating the more difficult puzzles and making sure that they were truly fair.

## The Answer

| Position | Vessel | Vial   | Property    |
| -------- | ------ | ------ | ----------- |
| 1        | Glass  | Orange | Translucent |
| 2        | Brass  | Black  | Acidic      |
| 3        | Copper | Purple | Alkaline    |
| 4        | Clay   | Green  | Viscous     |

---

## The Reasoning

**Step 1 — Anchor the ends.**

"Last is always the green, and always the clay." Both Green and Clay are position 4. No deduction needed; this is stated outright. It also tells you Clay is the only vessel that can hold Green without weeping — a material property, not flavor.

"First, the one you can see through." Glass is position 1. Glass is the only transparent vessel among the four.

**Step 2 — Extract properties from the vial descriptions.**

The vial descriptions are facts about the liquids, not ordering clues:

- _Orange_ — "thin, no smell, clear enough to read through" → Translucent
- _Black_ — "sharp bite," opens with warning → Acidic
- _Purple_ — "pungent, lingers" → Alkaline
- _Green_ — "moves slow, sweet, like warm honey" → Viscous

Green = Viscous, so position 4 also holds Viscous.

**Step 3 — Narrow position 2.**

"The second cup is not forgiving of curiosity." Fumes. The liquid in position 2 produces dangerous vapor — that means Acidic or Alkaline. Black (Acidic) or Purple (Alkaline) goes in the second cup.

**Step 4 — Crack position 3 with the blue.**

"It will blush before you've finished pouring the fourth. The blue won't spoil the compound."

The vessel develops a blue corrosion during the pour. Copper typically corrodes green (verdigris), but in the presence of an alkaline solution it forms a blue compound instead. The note is written matter-of-factly — the alchemist expects blue, because he knows what he's pouring. The vessel is Copper, and the liquid is Alkaline.

Alkaline = Purple. So Purple is position 3.

**Step 5 — Resolve position 2 by elimination.**

Purple is taken (position 3). Position 2 must hold the other fume-producing liquid: Black. Black = Acidic, so position 2 holds Acidic.

**Step 6 — Place Orange and Translucent.**

Orange is Translucent. It isn't in position 2 (Black is), position 3 (Purple is), or position 4 (Green is). Orange is position 1. Translucent is position 1.

**Step 7 — Place Brass.**

Glass = 1, Copper = 3, Clay = 4. The only vessel unplaced is Brass. Brass is position 2.

---

## The Trap

The blue is the misdirect. Copper corroding green is common knowledge — a solver who knows that will read "the blue" and pause. Either they reason through _why_ it's blue (alkaline reaction), which tells them both the vessel and the liquid in one step, or they carry the confusion forward and it resolves by elimination. Either way they get there. The alchemist never flags it as unusual, which is exactly the point — it isn't unusual to him.
